Women’s heart disease studies suggest lowering BP thresholds ~10 mmHg below men’s. The SPRINT sex‐specific analysis (Foy et al., J Hypertens 2018) showed no heterogeneity by sex with SBP targets <120 vs <140 for all
 – it did not set separate female cutoffs. In contrast, recent cohort analyses find women’s CVD risk rises at substantially lower SBP. Ji et al. (Circulation 2021) report that incident CVD appeared in women at SBP 100–109 mmHg versus 130–139 in men
. In their data, female SBP 100–109 produced an adjusted CVD hazard ratio ~1.25, comparable to the 1.26 seen in men at 130–139 (so women at ~105 mmHg equaled men at 130). Likewise, MI risk emerged at 110–119 in women vs ≥160 in men, and HF/stroke risks at 110–119 or 120–129 in women versus much higher SBP in men
. A review similarly notes that BP-linked ACS/HF risk begins ~10 mmHg lower in women, and that “high-normal” SBP (130–139) doubles MI risk in midlife women but not men
. Thus women’s BP zones should be shifted downward by ≈10 mmHg. For example, if men’s normal SBP is 120–129, then women’s normal would be ~110–119; women’s “high” (hypertension) would begin ~130 instead of 140. In practice this implies treating a woman with SBP ≈125 (vs a man at 135) as similarly “borderline” or high. In Ji’s data, women at ~105 mmHg had CVD risk equal to men at 130
. Based on these findings, we propose the table below.

Zone	Male SBP (mmHg)	Female SBP (mmHg)
Optimal	<120	<110
Normal	120–129	110–119
Borderline	130–139	120–129
High	≥140	≥130
